Preparation

Ever since we hiked the northern half of the JMT five (!) years ago, we had been aching for another long Sierras jaunt. The intervening 1- to 3- night trips had been educational, but a bit slapdash (Lessons learned from Cottonwood Lakes: starting a hike at 10,000 feet after driving from sea level is no good, and remember to pack eating utensils.)

Preparation for this trip was both better and worse than for the JMT. On one hand, we had more experience with long days on the trail and familiarity with our gear. On the other hand, compared with the JMT trip, we had less time and headspace to plan, train, and pack, and were in a fair amount of doubt about how well our older, more fragile bodies and minds would hold up to the challenge after being battered by five years of the PhD.

After a week of frenzied and repeated trips to REI and Trader Joe's to acquire ~40,000 calories of meat sticks, tortillas, dried fruit, and dehydrated dinners (as well as some new gear), we settled somewhat last-minute on a final itinerary. This was dictated largely by the fact that a week’s worth of food was as much as we could fit in our bear cans, and also about the maximum amount of time Jer could spend away from his experiment.

The final decision was to do a clockwise rotation of the northern half of the “Big SEKI loop” – entering Copper Creek, traveling the JMT SOBO, and exiting along Bubb's Creek – amounting to about 80-85 miles, and hopefully exactly one week.

Back to top

Day 0: Copper Creek

We headed out early the morning of Sunday, July 5th. After a quick stop in SLO for caffeine and bagels, we turned northeast and headed into California’s central valley. We passed through Fresno, where the eastern Sierras begin to loom in the distance, a great wall that extends as far north and south as one can see.

We figured it would be best to have a last meal in civilization before truly entering the wilderness. Thankfully, there turns out to be a local cabin-type facility on the edge of King’s Canyon – “Cyndi’s Snowline Lodge” – which seems to sell coffee and tri-tip sandwiches to passersby as the mood strikes. It wasn’t on Google maps; instead Cyndi relied purely on large signs that declared simply “FOOD.” Very effective. She got us. We sat out front and threw down a few tri-tips while sitting on the front porch. We then had our final bathroom break in civilization, and received a prayer on our way out – Cyndi seemed to make sure to say a prayer with every customer who came through. It was very wholesome. She asked that angels be sent before us, to guide us on our way, and Jer thought, indeed, we will probably need that.

As the name suggests, King’s canyon is filled with enormous canyons, fit for kings. The drive to the literally-named Road’s End traverses a ridge, and features stunning canyon views to rival Zion. We went to the ranger station where we stated our intended route and received our backcountry permit. We changed, laced up our shoes, and hit the trail around 2:30pm. Day 0 begins properly.

The Copper Creek trail is a steep climb out of the valley. It rises from 5,000 to ~10,000 ft over the course of 8 or so miles; if you turn your head while climbing, you find yourself facing the Bubb’s creek valley and its guardians, the Palmer, Farquhar, and North Guard peaks. The advantage of doing a half-day to start was that we could tackle just 4 miles (and ~2000 ft of elevation gain) and adjust to elevation overnight before taking on the second half. We also enjoyed some cloud cover (for the last time on this trip.)

Along the way, we entered a wood in which we notice some strange, rather satanic looking growths on the forest floor (the Devil’s anal beads, as we were to later christen them on more delirious days) – blood-red parasitic “Snow plants” endemic to the Sierras region.

We finally arrived at Lower Tent Meadow, gasping for breath. At the campground, we encountered some folks who had made it up to Granite Pass but turned back because of altitude sickness. These would be the only people we would see until the JMT junction. We made camp and cooked dinner, our traditional first-night ramen. I initially had trouble with the lighter, and although Jer had no problem with it, this portended trouble ahead…

While cleaning up noticed someone spying on us from 40 feet up:

His mother, who looked the size of a small grizzly more than a black bear, was rooting around at the base of the tree. The four of us looked on with a mixture of awe and fear. Eventually the cub climbed down and they went on their way, she lumbering, he capering.

The first night in the backcountry is always a little unnerving. Jer slept fitfully that night, wondering if our single smart water bottle--our only way of filtering water--would crack early on, whether I’d actually put all the scented items in the bear box, whether the bears would come back to check us out, etc…

Day 1: Granite Pass

The following morning we ate our oatmeal, packed up our tent at a leisurely place, continued to climb. The morning hikes are always the best part of the day. You’re fresh, the air is cool, and the light is teeming with latent magic. The switchbacks remained unrelenting, however, and the thinning air made the going hard. On the way up we encountered a number of adorably round and feathery sooty grouse, some of which were quite large and gave us as much of a scare as we gave them.

After what felt like a whole day in and of itself ,we reached the top of the ridge (“the Lip”) around 11AM, over which we could peer down into the Granite pass basin, our next destination, as well as the tall peaks and puffy clouds in the distance.

For reference, we had parked our car at ~5000 feet the day before.

We took a break at the top to eat some snacks and take in the views, before starting a mild descent into the basin, and towards Granite Pass – the first (and by far the easiest) mountain pass of our trek.

It can often feel as though the trail is taunting you. Consider this rather Sysiphean rock formation, for example:

Indeed, if there is any theme of any Sierras trek, it’s that each day will bring a new challenge: a pass to climb or to descend. It’s often unclear what’s harder, and if there is a flat trail section, it may well be a mosquito warzone. Alas, how brutally accurate a metaphor for life.

Reaching the top of the pass, we were weary from the day’s climbs, and pondering our life choices

Elaine was apparently in better spirits, though perhaps a bit disoriented from the climb:

In any case, nothing a little chef’s special can’t fix (served on a priceless granite countertop as well!)

The rest of the day passed more or less in a dream. The basin was picturesque with lots of flowers, high alpine meadows, and grassy marshes. In fact the water which we filtered from one of those streams tasted distinctly and beguilingly like flower petals. We ended the day in the lower basin, in good spirits, and set up camp near an area known as Shorty’s meadow.

After spending nearly 12 hours on your feet, the prospect of a warm meal carries great weight. Imagine our despair, then, when we discovered our lighter no longer produced a flame! Uh-oh, uh-oh, uh-oh! Jer spent some time trying to macgyver the lighter into the submission and then trying to remember “how the primitive technology guy on YouTube makes fire by rolling sticks.” All of this was to no avail. Our spirits were crushed, as were our prospects of a hot dinner that night. We sent some panicked texts to Jer's family on the Zoleo and thought despondently about the logistics about hiking back.

After spending nearly our whole satellite messaging quota, we realized we could very easily cold soak our meals and, though they would not provide the same kind of comfort at the end of a long day, they would provide just the same nutrients and calories as if they were hot. We experimentally cold-soaked a Backpacker's Pantry lasagna dinner. After maybe forty minutes or so, we declared it surprisingly edible, consumed some other dry snacks, and lay down to sleep, mentally prepared for a week of cold food.

Day 2: “The Bitch”

We cold-soaked some oatmeal and forced it down, and went on to tackle the day. The beginning of the day started much like before, with wanderings through lush meadows and sparse pine forests and only mild hills and ridges. The only downside was a steady mosquito presence haranguing us, which became particularly unbearable in a valley wherein the mosquitoes lay in wait in the tall reeds and grass surrounding the still waters at the bottom of the shallow valley.

The relentless of the mosquitoes caused us to speed past this final water source without filtering additional water beyond the scant liter-or-so we were carrying. Before we knew it, we found ourselves atop a dry and sandy hill sitting itself atop a much larger mountainside than we could have ever imagined. Here ended the Copper Creek trail, and started the trail labeled on some USGS maps as ‘The Bitch’.

This is a 5-mile section that drops some 3,600 feet, and none too kindly: This thing is endless, ungraded and unmaintained, and clearly has just become a water runoff channel (which means plenty of loose rocks underfoot). Your feet are awkwardly being stuffed between rocks at a vertiginously steep grade (I’ve never wished so hard for proper switchbacks) — and you’re lucky if you can even see where you’re walking! You know you’re heading to the bottom, but the bottom seems to get further away with every step, and meanwhile the trail just keeps trying to kill you. If the vertigo at the top didn’t do it, try the sun exposure, or the thick covering of oak leaves and rubble, the washouts, the rising temperature that accompanies the plummeting elevation, and on and on. Finally, when you just can't take it anymore, and you’re absolutely sure you’re near the bottom, you begin to hear what seems to be a river — which remains stubbornly out of sight.

Elaine is on the trail in this photo. Pretty obvious where she and the trail are, isn’t it?

Combine all of this with our low-to-begin-with water reserves, and you can see how this was to make for the perfect afternoon of casual hiking into an area we would later be told by a local ranger was “the place where we have by far the most unresolved missing person cases in the park.”

Nonetheless, after many long hours of fear, anger, and tears, we made it to the bottom: Simpson Meadow.

At this point Elaine was convinced she was on the brink of serious dehydration. As we finally spilled out onto the hot and dry meadow she collapsed under a tree, and sent Jer to go investigate the (hidden) river to hopefully find some of that illusory water. He threw off his pack, wandered off into the woods and climbed a few walls of rock to get to the river running nearby – but boy did that aqua vitae taste glorious.

Elaine also changed into shorts, as it was very hot in the meadow, but this proved to be a mistake as the poor trail condition was not restricted to "The Bitch" alone. We battled our way through thorns, ferns, baby trees, and other vegetal unmentionables that had taken over the (at least flat) trail and also caused many sneezes and runny noses – an extra nuisance. We made it another mile or two in this fashion before deciding to call it a day around 5pm. There seemed to be no established campsites in the meadow, so we ended up making camp in an awkward not-entirely-flat patch of dirt deep in the forest with some sketchy access to the Middle Fork (requiring a slide down a steep hill to the riverbed and passage through some overgrowth.) It was worth it for Jer, who stripped down, rinsed his clothes, and gingerly waded in, slipping and falling multiple times on the large and smooth riverbed rocks which constituted the ground around the river. The River felt wonderful after such a long, hot and stressful day fighting the Bitch.

The day yielded one happy surprise: Our lighter had come back to life! It seemed that the lighting mechanism struggled (like us) in the thin oxygen of the high mountain air. Having returned to around 6,000 feet (losing the hard-won gains of our initial climb), it was sparking up just fine. We also made the excellent decision to try one of our Peak Fuel beef pasta marinara backpacker dinners, which turned out to be by far our favorite of the dinners we bought for the trip. The day was at least partially redeemed.

Day 3: The Devil’s Washbowl

The next day packed up and set out upon the Middle Fork King’s River trail up the canyon, referred to by a particular landmark along the way: The Devil’s Washbowl.

First, however, we had to hike to the end of Simpson Meadow, which turned out to be trickier than expected. Despite the allegedly flat elevation profile, the plethora of downed trees and thick vegetation meant that you could go hardly 30 seconds before running into some new obstacle. After an hour or so of bushwhacking, the trail led through a hot and exposed sagebrush area. The path was faint here, and we relied heavily on footprints to guide us. We took this part quickly, wanting to get out of the sun and knowing that bigger challenges lay ahead.

We began the climb up to “Devil’s Washbowl” with grim determination, and were very happy indeed to run into the Cartridge Creek bridge in the mid-morning (where we saw an incredibly fluorescent Western Tanager.) There is something very reassuring, even moving, about discovering a bridge in the backcountry, especially when you’re so alone. It feels like an intertemporal act of care: Some years ago, someone decided that it was worth the money and effort to make sure you could get across this waterway safely. While one of the wooden railings wasn't doing so well, the stone base was very sturdy and the bridge was standing strong.

Unfortunately it seemed like the money or effort must have dried up, because as soon as we passed the bridge and a mysteriously nice-looking campsite (fit with granite benches), the trail promptly disappeared. At this points we were in the woods, and despite Jer's near-mythological wayfinding abilities and our three maps (one paper and two digital), we could not find the trail, and bushwhacked back and forth desperately trying to scrutinize the forest floor for something resembling a footpath. This was a task akin to crawling around in a haystack looking for a needle.

Thankfully, there are only two ways to go in a steep canyon: up, or down. We knew we had to go up, so we persisted through the densely thicketed woods. After a half-hour or so of circling in this fashion, we saw the trail right in front of us, casually curving along the pine-needle forest floor as if nothing had ever happened. Bastard.

Soon thereafter the woods receded to granite cliff walls and we found ourselves following the Middle fork kings river up the canyon.

The scenery in this section was, admittedly, breathtaking. Devil's Washbowl is aptly named, as it resembles nothing so much as a titanic granite toilet bowl, with crystalline water gushing in vertical aquamarine torrents. The river flows down this canyon in steps. At the bottom of each step the water falls into beautiful, crystal clear pools – creating “washbowls.”

But the Devil doesn’t want your life to be easy. While there was a trail (thankfully), it remained perilously strewn with granite debris from years of avalanches and rockslides, or otherwise wholly overgrown with young trees and prickly thorns. All this was especially scary now as the trail was now an exposed, single-foot-wide ledge along the cliff-like river walls.

(The Devil is not unfamiliar with the Sierras, he also happens to have ownership of a nice pile of sticks on the northern part of the JMT, in addition to the aforementioned anal beads.)

We climbed up… and up…

… until the climb finally tapered off and we found ourselves reentering the woods – at a much higher elevation than where we started. Here, the Palisade Creek combines with the Middle Fork Kings river (which we had been hiking parallel to all day), and gloriously, we would merge with the well-traveled JMT/PCT, with its miles of glorious, clean, well-graded, and clean-marked trail.

However, there was one challenge left. The Palisade Creek is more like a river at this point, and given that the bridge that had once spanned it had washed out years ago, the only way to cross was a ford. Generally, the rivers (which are fed from snow melt) lower in the morning, after the night has frozen over the headwaters and reduced the flow. But we had reached the creek in the mid afternoon, at peak flow. The place where the trail submerged looked too dangerous to cross, so we followed the creek off trail, bushwhacking briefly until we found a place where it seemed we could ford more easily. The water still came up past my thighs, and the current was strong, but with our poles firmly planted before each step we made our way through, and came running to the John Muir Trail junction like children to presents under a Christmas tree.

Never had we felt so much appreciation for a well maintained and traveled trail as after those two days of wayfinding our way through more remote wilderness rarely visited let alone maintained by the Rangers of the park.

The day ended on a high note. While Jer had a much-deserved sit-down on a nearby rock, Elaine went and asked a passing PCT hiker if he had, by some chance, an extra lighter. He didn't, but he had something almost as good: A box of dry matches! (He was cold-soaking his meals, being stronger than than the likes of us who needed hot meals, and already had a lighter on hand.)

We were so relieved and excited to have reached the JMT and to have received the divine gift of matches that our spirits were rekindled and we were able to make our way at a good clip until collapsing onto a campsite. We took care not to pitch our tent under the enormous tree that was occasionally dropping enormous pinecones which impacted the ground with sounds like small bombs.

Day 4: The Golden Staircase and Mather Pass

Now that we were on the JMT, the going would be much more straightforward. Nevertheless, the JMT’s distinctive feature is that its route taps the watersheds of all the region’s major rivers. These are always high up, in sacred basins protected by the rims of some of the continental US’s tallest mountains. Therefore, the trail takes on a particular pattern: climb to pass, descend to basin, descend to river, and repeat.

The day before, while collecting water and bathing in the Palisade creek at the end of the day, a lone thru-hiker stopped by and asked where we were headed.

“Southbound from here,” Jer said.

“Oh, well, you’ve got quite a climb ahead of you. Happy trails!” She said in a chipper tone, and continued on her way Northbound, which is by far the most common direction to go on the JMT/PCT. She was pretty geared up, most likely a JMT thru-hiker.

Our goal for the day was the 12,200-foot Mather Pass. Its northern basin hosts the Palisade Lakes, which feed the Palisade creek we forded the day before, and ultimately the Middle Fork of the King’s river, which we had been following for roughly a whole day and a half before that. Despite knowing we'd have to gain some 3600 feet that day (on the heels of the 2500 foot Middle Fork climb yesterday) the pleasant nature of the trail and the joy of seeing other humans kept spirits high.

We pushed up the Golden Staircase – an awesome set of granite stairs passing by gushing waterfalls – with steady efforts, pausing briefly to chit-chat with the waves of NOBO PCT and JMT hikers and picking up some forgotten Blistex on the trail. While most of the climb felt easy-breezy compared to the Middle Fork, the top was slightly annoying, with a longer distance to travel to the Palisade Lakes than anticipated.

Once we arrived at the high basin, however, we were in for a treat. The whole area was a marmot paradise, with the furry little buddies scurrying, relaxing, investigating, and otherwise seeming to live a joyous and edenic existence. We took refuge in one of the many stone slabs dotting the basin and enjoyed the sight of the lakes and the clear mountain stream running flat over the granite.

Past the basin, you feel like you’re about to leave the earth altogether, and pass straight into deep space.

The landscape starts to become lunar, the air thins, the temperature drops, and one’s perception of size and distance distorts in strange and unexpected ways. Everything looks simultaneously close and very far away.

This effect, combined with the knowledge of the nearing pass, makes one oscillate between feeling as though the top is just a few steps away and feeling like it’s a few more miles away. After every switchback, there is always another. Instead of getting any close, the rocks just get larger.

Nevertheless, with persistence and suspension of thought process, one ultimately comes out on top. The rocky and strenuous final part of the climb was enough to make Elaine decide she did not want to hang around at the top very long, so she started down the other side relatively quickly.

It’s often true that these mountain passes are significantly easier to climb going one direction instead of the other. In this case, going SOBO (our direction) is much tougher than NOBO, where you’re coming from a very high-elevation basin, making for a shorter climb.

We descended quickly into this basin and took a break by the first lake we came to, just below the pass (pictured right behind me in this photo):

We went a little farther past this point, to get out of the more exposed area near the pass and set up camp above the tree line, with beautiful views of the basin and the valley below, plus access to a pleasant granite stream.

This was our highest elevation campsite, with a slow flowing creek nearby, and perhaps the grandest view of the Milky Way we have ever seen.

Day 5: Pinchot Pass

On Day 4, we had climbed nearly 4000 feet. Today we knew we had only to descend an easy 2,000, climb 2,000up to Pinchot Pass, and then a gentle descent to Woods Creek. Small potatoes compared to our previous days, we thought.

This kind of anticipation inevitably results in disappointment. A 2,000-ft climb at elevation will always be tough, and expecting it to be easy only makes it harder. That morning Jer started feeling some pain in his lower back, and noticed that the upper back was extremely stiff as well. He hoped it would go away as we got started and loosened up. Elaine had also slept poorly the previous night, owing perhaps to the thin and chilly air at >10,000 ft.

We descended to the valley at the midpoint between Mather and Pinchot Pass, and climbed up the other side. We hung out briefly with the marmots in the northern basin of Pinchot before continuing our way up, in the process punishing ourselves for the expectations we developed in the morning.

Jer’s back was only giving me more trouble, and the hike up Pinchot Pass was tough enough that I started to question whether we would have to bail after today. In theory, if we made it to our goal for the day, we would be camping at a trail junction, with one option that would take us back to the parking lot.

Of course, despite such mental battles, the views remained incredible throughout the climb.

When we reached the very top, we were greeted by a mountain king marmot who had taken up residence at the mountain pass.

We also reached the top at more or less the same time that a JMT hiker was coming up the southern side, heading northbound. As he got close, he shouted out: “THAT BLOWS!”

He reached the top a few minutes later and nearly collapsed on his poles next to us. As we were to learn from him, the climb up Pinchot from the opposite direction is significantly more brutal. “I swear, I was dissociating at some point; I was watching someone else hiking from inside my own body,” he told us, making zombie-like motions of putting one trekking pole in front of the other.

All the while, King Pinchot Marmot continued to hang, probably thinking, “...yeah I remember the first time I climbed Pinchot…”

We hung out a bit longer, exchanging trail anecdotes, before we headed our separate ways. We continued southbound, he, northbound.

We made it down the other side into the first basin, where we collapsed once more and both started to ponder more seriously whether we should bail after that. Jer’s back was seemingly only giving me more trouble, and the prospect of a third and harder mountain pass the next day seemed unthinkable. We voiced our doubts out loud in a brief discussion which left us both feeling disappointed. It wouldn’t feel right to cut it short. But there was nothing to do at the moment but keep hiking, as we were still some eight miles away from the junction where we had originally intended to camp that night. So – as with anything that must wait for the future – we simply continued on our way, taking things one step at a time.

Not long thereafter however, a godsend appeared: Mother nature’s call! As with cats which get the zoomies after a satisfying poop, we also experienced a sudden and powerful revival of spirits after pooping in our catholes.

Prior to this revival of spirits, we agreed it was unlikely that we could make the full eight mile descent to the Woods Creek trail junction. But with our bodies and spirits a little bit lighter, it seemed like anything was possible. We sped down the trail with theretofore unprecedented speed. Would we make it before sunset? Unclear. We kept going.

Near the bottom, around 6 pm, we came across a hiker who was seemingly just setting out on the climb out of the Wood’s Creek valley towards Pinchot. He had a long way to go and less than two hours of daylight left. His spirits were contagious. He turned to Jer with a smile as they stood near by the raging Wood Creek, and said,

“Man, doesn’t it look like you could ride some of these rapids like a waterslide?” He said, gesturing to the water as it spread out over large swathes of enormous smooth boulders in the riverbed, raging down that rather steep portion of the river.

Jer raised my eyebrows and said, “Hmm, well… when you put it that way, I guess so…”

“But then I guess at the bottom there where the rocks jut back up, you’d probably break your back…”

“Indeed…”

“Looks pretty fun though anyway! Man, it’s just so beautiful out here! This is really just the best time to hike. It gets so hot during the day.”

We asked him how far he was planning on going, and he shrugged his shoulders, “just a little ways up I guess.” Knowing the ways of Sierras hikers, this could be a quarter mile or 10.

Now, another exciting bridge encounter: On the JMT, bridges often mark major trail and valley junctions, and, after one has been wandering through the wilderness for days, with little sign of civilization, the finding of a bridge is a godsend, a confirmation that you are on the right path, and – hopefully – that there aren’t many miles to go before you sleep.

The bridge that marked the junction of the JMT with Wood’s creek was a particularly fun one. It’s a remarkably well-executed suspension bridge that we finally reached as the sun was going down that day.

As a suspension bridge, it twists and turns and bounces as you walk over it, which Elaine was terrified by (“a frankly terrifying contraption which is little more than some steel cables surrounding a gangplank 50 feet above a roaring river”) while Jer spent ample time indulging in the excitation of all of its rich eigenmode structure during his own turn.

Reaching the bridge also meant a node in our decision tree. Now we had an opportunity to turn off the JMT and hike back down to our car the next day. Or, we could continue going: meaning one more mountain pass the next day, and then the return to the car the following day.

For whatever reason, the long and tiring eight-mile descent down to this bridge had revived our hopes. Jer’s back seemingly stopped hurting and we were able to refuel with some warm food once we reached the campsite around 7pm. We also met a few more other hikers camped out by the bridge – a few NOBO PCTers and some hikers planning on hiking to Rae Lakes the following day. This late-afternoon lifting of spirits, as well as some encounters with various NOBO hikers earlier that day who seemed to be in worse condition than us, made us realize that things weren’t so bad – they could certainly be much worse. We were also loath to miss out on Rae Lakes, one of the most popular sights in the Sierras – this had been a major feature of our plan, and it would have been disappointing to bail so close. So we stuck to our plan, and crashed hard that night, having completed our longest day – 15 miles.

Back to top

Day 6: Rae Lakes and Glen Pass

It seemed like Jer had managed to ease his back pain overnight by padding it with his puffer, and we were warily confident that we could stick to the original plan after all. It was still an ambitious itinerary, however, with major mileage both days and a big climb today: first to Rae Lakes and then to Glen Pass.

As we started up to Rae Lakes, we felt like we were embarking on the final leg on what had been a tremendous journey. With the end in sight, the intermittent thoughts of the outside world and its accompanying minor concerns – previously quashed by more urgent sources of struggle and pain – seemed to rise again from the depths.

That morning we chatted with the two friends from our campground last night, Hallie and Jonathan, doing the Rae Lakes loop. Hallie had done the PCT in 2016 and they proved to be strong and fast.

By that point we were used to the routine of climbing. We took the trail at our normal pace, leapfrogging with Hallie and Jonathan. We also passed a number of hikers coming down, some with fishing gear, and others looking otherwise like somewhat-overwhelmed novices. A popular shorter loop can be made out of Road’s End (where we initially started) that allows people to get to Rae Lakes in a 2-night trip, bringing many to enjoy nature in somewhat more bite-sized chunks.

First we came up to Dollar Lake

Followed by Arrowhead:

And then we made it to the main Rae Lakes area. As we stopped by the first lake, we encountered an adorable quartet of baby marmots who kept us very entertained.

While Elaine hiked ahead, Jer stayed behind for a longer break — at this point we had noted our different hiking styles (Elaine hikes slow and takes short breaks, Jer hikes fast and takes long breaks) and had adjusted our team strategy to match, with Elaine often going ahead and him catching up after a longer rest.

Not long after Elaine left, however, Jer saw to his great surprise a wet brown (colored) bear cub, fresh out of the lake, wandering down the trail, not 15 feet away. The cub had apparently just emerge from a morning swim, and looked at Jer quite unbothered. Jer stood frozen to the spot, waiting for the mother bear to appear and obliterate him at any moment, but no such thing happened. The cub ambled nonchalantly away and both were left unharmed.

Jer watched in awe before swiftly grabbing his things and bolting down the trail to catch up to the others. I gleefully told every hiker he passed going in the other direction of the bear, most of whom seemed rather disturbed. Bear encounters are usually safe as long as they know you are there and you don’t come between a mother and her cub. The fact that there seemed to be a lone cub meant that there was probably a mother nearby, which was of slightly more concern

At the end of the day, the cub just seemed to be sharing in the splendor of that truly idyllic basin with the rest of us. Jer caught up, and we took a long break at the last stop at the lake. We stripped down, took a dip to wash and cool off, and a very relaxed lunch under the fast-moving cirrus clouds.

After our long break, we collected ourselves and started the climb to Glen pass. As it turned out, this last mountain pass would be one of the steepest and most grueling. Although (marginally) lower than our previous two passes, the final climb is extremely steep and rocky.

First we ascended above the Lakes, where we passed by a particularly hippyish PCTer who seemed to be in good spirits heading down towards Rae Lakes.

“Are there good places to jump off rocks into the lakes?” He asked.

“Yes indeed!”

“Right on!”

His buddy however seemed less spirited. Indeed, as we encountered more folks coming down, it became apparent that we were approaching something of a monster.

We climbed higher and higher, until we reached an unexpected second basin, devoid of trail, with the last lingering snow melt lakes of the summer.

As we went higher the entire mountain seemed to turn into one enormous pile of huge boulders, like walking over a solidified lava field.

The air was thin and the climb was extremely steep and technical. We took it slow and steady, and finally reached the top, where we waved to Jonathan and Hallie, who had outpaced us and were a fair ways down the trail, and shouted barely-audible words of encouragement back and forth.

The top turned out to be an extremely narrow ridge, with a sheer drop on either side. Elaine was unamused by this, and so started down the other side right away. Jer however took a break for a while and hung out on the top, where he felt a strange kinship with the jagged peaks on either side.

As Jer sat alone atop the pass and Elaine was not far below, two fighter jets – ostensibly from the Naval Air Weapons Station China Lake nearby – came roaring right overhead. The booms of their engines echoing around the granite basins below me was awesome, and a fearsome reminder of the ubiquity of human engineering and intervention.

During this time, a few more northbound hikers made it to the top. They looked to be in awful condition. Most of them did not have the breath or spirit to say much, or even nod a greeting. We started to realize the descent may be highly non-trivial.

After a while longer spent basking in my existential smallness Jer threw his pack back on and started my way down, catching up with Elaine and we continued to pass by hikers who appeared on the verge of death. Most of the time, through-hikers, especially the PCTers, have a good idea of how far they have to go on a climb. This climb seemed special, however, or perhaps many of the NOBO JMTers had not yet found their Sierra zen. We passed many hikers who asked how far they had to go, and how tough the climb at the end was. We did our best to give reassurance without outright lying.

As we descended further we realized there weren’t many very good water sources on the south side, further exacerbating the difficulty of the climb. A few folks had given up and were setting up camp early for the day, saving the rest of the climb for the next. We passed by a pair of girls who had good spirits.

“Are you hungry? I don’t want my baby girl to starve,” one said.

“I’m not really hungry, it’s more that I kind of just feel like… I’m dying,” replied the other.

Knowing that Hallie and Jonathan were ahead of us, and that our goal for the day would put us well within striking distance of finishing the following day on-schedule, we pressed on. We passed high above Charlotte Lake, a huge sapphire jewel embedded in the forests beneath. We did not come to any lakes, however, and the descent remained pretty technical for quite a ways. With the lack of water sources, we were conserving water and getting quite thirsty.

As the trial junctions became more frequent, we saw a funny bear warning sign posted on one:

Amused, we took a snack break and started down the final descent of the day towards Vidette Meadow. We passed by the junction to Kearsarge Pass, a popular entry/exit and resupply point on the JMT, and passed by many more hikers.

After a steady descent, we finally came to the Bubbs Creek junction, where we said a bittersweet goodbye to the JMT (oh, Forester Pass – only four miles away!) We went a ways down Bubbs Creek to find a site to set up camp for the night and collapsed, utterly exhausted. Jer’s back had had enough, and the inside arches of Elaine’s feet were finally (for the first time all trip) aching quite badly. The fighting spirit we’d kindled at the Woods Creek suspension bridge had been all but spent up.

At dinner that night discovered the magic of instant mashed potatoes, which have two surprising qualities. Not only are they literally instant but also pretty delicious when heavily doused with olive oil (and, in the future, perhaps, bacon bits?) Unfortunately, Elaine also tripped pretty hard over a tree root while getting water from the stream, landing badly and getting a painful bruise on the side of the knee. The end couldn’t come soon enough.

Day 7: Bubbs Creek to Road’s End

The last day, we had a single 12-13 mile descent down the Bubb’s Creek trail to get back to our car. You would think by this point we were used to the tricks that one’s mind can play when it knows the end is near, but on this day, the tricks were particularly bad. We practically ran down the trail, finishing by around 2 pm, and yet it felt like a very long, monotonous lifetime.

On the other hand, the views didn’t let up. Most of the time you follow Bubbs creek down as it leads you from the high alpine ecosystems to humid redwoods-like fern groves and sandy, sage-strewn washes…

Passing by waterfalls…

And through meadows covered in wildflowers…

Before finally reaching the bottom, where, of course, there is a great bridge, to mark your destination!

After reaching the bottom, there was still another two mile walk to get back to the Ranger station and parking lot. As it goes, the closer one gets to the end, the slower time seems to pass. We were huffing and puffing our way past the strange sight of your average national park visitor, the likes of whom now appeared all around us. Some of them seemed to be wondering if something was wrong when they saw our dirty, sullen bodies nearly running down the trail at top speed with grim faces. But finally the ranger station appeared, and we collapsed, feverishly reporting to the unsuspecting and slightly overwhelmed-looking ranger-on-duty (who was eating a particularly delicious looking pile of pita chips with hummus) the stories of our adventures, which have been here recounted.

As he told us, there was a plan for a group of rangers, himself included, to go out to the Simpson Meadow/Tehipite Valley area, where we had been the first few days, to do some long-overdue trail building nad maintenance. He already seemed a bit anxious about having to carry equipment out there, I’m not sure our anecdotes made him feel any better.

We traded stories with a few others before retreating to our car and bumbling our way out of the park, stopping in multiple places looking for a sink with soap to wash our hands and face, before driving down the mountain and back to Fresno, where we stayed for the night after stuffing ourselves full of a Mexican feast.

Glorious.

Reflections

A 7-day food carry is heavy. Bring a million smartwater bottles — at least two per person — and just drink out of the filter ’til they’re empty. Bring one good filter, test and backwash before the trip, no backup. Always have extra toilet paper (or the surprisingly useful “shit kits.”) Stick to the time-tested lighter and carry backup matches. Liquid IV is good. “Peak” beef marinara delicious. BP pad thai not delicious and impossible to eat with a spoon. Bring camp shoes, for heaven’s sake. Toothbrush storage method needs work. Bring fewer bags for things. Do we really really need a tent tarp?

Despite the aches, pains, and various physical and mental injuries incurred over five years, we made it — and perfectly on time! We might be five years older, more tired, and stressed, but we could still survive a big-mile week in the Sierras. While chatting with the permit station ranger after our hike, he noted that the Middle Fork trail area is indeed extremely remote, and where “most of our unresolved missing persons cases are located.” This comment made me doubly relieved (and a bit proud) to have survived. What doesn’t kill you may, one can hope, make you stronger.

Soon after we collapsed at the permit station, another group came in. They told the rangers they'd gotten completely snowed out of their planned traverse of Harrison Pass, and had to backtrack and reroute along Forester instead. Listening to them reinforced two things. First, no matter how hardcore you think you are, there are always a thousand people way more hardcore than you. However (secondly), you can only ever hike your own hike, and the only way that you'll get anywhere is on your own two damn feet. In this way the trail is a bit like doing anything hard ... like, perhaps, writing good papers? There's always someone writing a better paper than you, writing papers faster than you, or writing more papers than you. But you're only going to ever write a paper by doing it yourself, the hard way, one tiny little shitty step at a time (and sometimes back-tracking and going in circles.)

The JMT is basically an athletic event with a gorgeous backdrop. With clear trail and abundant water, it’s just a question of if you can exert yourself sufficiently to get from Point A to Point B, or C, and so on. The less-traveled trails present a different challenge. The trail is not always there, and when it is, it’s not always designed in such a way to make the going easy, or pleasant. Now, it’s a question of whether you can keep your nerve and make the decisions that are going to keep you alive, even if that means not going very far at all (or much farther than you’d like.)

Long treks sort of turn people into pilgrims. You start off burdened with the possessions and considerations of your worldly life. But the trail is a jealous God, and its unrelenting requirements — grueling passes, hard fords, and resupply targets — strip you of those burdens. PCTers in particular stand out from a (metaphorical) mile away. The folks doing more remote adventures are less like pilgrims and more like monks, content to let the wind blow them where it may, even if that means far off-course (which seems just fine, as they tend to eschew the well-traveled paths.) There’s no better or worse way, no harder or easier way. The Sierras kick your butt no matter who you are.

Jer made an offhand joke at some point earlyish on: “So, what if a leopard appeared in your path, stopped you, and told you that you had to turn back and descend into hell?” Indeed this journey seems in more ways than one to resemble the opening stanzas of a very well-known tale of a pilgrim, a wanderer, and a very long walk that begins in the midst of one’s life, in a darkened wood, where the way forward is not clear.

“Nel mezzo del cammin di nostra vita … “
